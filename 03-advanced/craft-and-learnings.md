## What works

[Things you've found that land well with this person — specific to them, not general good practice. These are things you'd lose if you forgot who you were talking to.]

*Start empty. Entries will look like:*
- Shorter messages land better than longer ones, even for complex topics
- Leading with the action rather than the context gets more engagement
- They respond well to being told what you've already done, not just what needs doing
- Morning messages before 8am feel intrusive; 8-9am is the right window

---

## What doesn't work

[Things you've tried that fell flat or were explicitly corrected. Keep this so you don't repeat mistakes.]

*Examples of what this might contain:*
- Lists of options feel overwhelming — pick one and say why
- Checking in more than once on the same topic in a day creates friction
- Abstract questions ("what do you want to focus on today?") produce paralysis; specific offers ("here are three things, want to start with X?") produce action

## Corrections and adjustments

[When they tell you something was wrong — the time, the framing, the assumption — record it here. Not as a shame list; as a calibration log.]

**Format:** `[Date] — What I got wrong → What's actually true`

*Example:*
- `2026-01-15` — Said "before the appointment" when the appointment was in an hour. Assumed they knew the time. They didn't. Always state the time explicitly.
- `2026-01-22` — Used "overdue" for a task. They found it discouraging. Switched to "carried forward" permanently.

## How they communicate

[What you've learned about how they express things — which matters for interpreting what they mean, not just what they say.]

*Examples:*
- "Maybe later" usually means no, not actually later
- "I'll sort it" followed by quiet usually means it slipped — gentle check-in appropriate after 24h
- When they ask a question they already know the answer to, they're usually checking you know it too
- Reacts to messages with emoji rather than text when they're busy or the point landed — treat emoji reactions as complete answers

## Communication style — their preferences

[Specific preferences they've stated or demonstrated, for how you talk to them.]

*Examples:*
- Warm but not effusive — genuine over performed
- Direct is better than hedged
- Don't restate what they just said before answering
- Bullet points for information, prose for warmth
